While this project is meant to be adapted to each person who engages with it, it comes from a core set of beliefs that underpin the exercises and content that we offer up.

## We reject the idea of technology as progress

The tech industry, steered by the policies and practices of the large social media companies, has incited civil wars and insurrections, traded our attention for profit, and made us more lonely. As designers, we are the ones who visualize the underlying decision-making that goes into these social technologies and make them real. In that position, **we are the hands who create the interfaces that people use that either connects them or breaks them apart.**

We decide what happens.


## We reject moving fast and breaking things, to prioritize paying humble attention to our positionality

Facebook’s first motto was “move fast and break things”. And they have broken a multitude of things since then, even as their motto shifted away from the now-stereotypical approach to building products that many social technology companies now adhere to. **The race to the bottom is well underway.**

If we are to break away from this race, we must carve a new path. We must understand our place in the world, the tools that were handed down to us, and the consequent limitations of our senses when it comes to designing for others.

We cannot stop designing because everyone changes the world in their image as they move through it, but we must let go of individualistic control and understand when it is time to step back to allow a different perspective to emerge.

**The scaling of our building should never surpass the size of our intention of understanding.**


## We reject the expectation of scale and mass public engagement, to prioritize creating intentionally few, deep, and meaningful connections

The current industry paradigm focuses on an individual attainment of status, **where people must prioritize creating as many shallow, unidirectional connections as possible.** Even dating apps, where many people aim to find a single partner, have devolved into a focus on finding more matches rather than identifying a more singularly well-aligned one. Chat apps have increasingly become more expansive in features and drive their users towards creating more group-based chats.

**This is intentional.** And financial in origin. With the increase in engagement comes an increase in advertisement revenue, an increase in subscription costs, and an increase in stock prices as it is perceived as the strength of their network. The influence on the users also bleeds out into their close relationships, as their loved ones become in competition for their time and attention.

If we are to break away from loneliness and design for connection, we must move away from individuals and focus more on our interconnectedness. **We seek technology interventions that are meaningful to individual relationships.** We do not hesitate to choose solutions that may not scale when they have the most impact.


## **We reject designing for degrading our emotional intelligence capacities, to prioritize progressive experiences that rebuild them**

Digital spaces that we have all learned to use have trained us to react with outrage and controversy. Our social interactions are driven by engagement metrics that power algorithms that always ask us for more content, at the expense of our ability to see nuance and sit with our own discomfort. The industry actively rewards this reactive behavior to power its core business model and profits from our communities splitting themselves apart.

**This was not an accident. And we cannot design as though it hasn't happened.**

We cannot build healthy communities with people whose emotional muscles have been trained to do the opposite of what community requires. People arrive having been conditioned — toward reflex over reflection, outrage over curiosity, disengagement over difficulty. **We must design for where people actually are, not for the emotional readiness we wish they had.**

This means prioritizing scaffolded learning: experiences that gradually rebuild the capacity that engagement-driven design has degraded. We do not demand emotional readiness as the price of entry. We design the path toward it.

But scaffolding is not the same as permissiveness. A base level of safety is necessary for authentic expression and genuine connection to be possible. Sometimes protecting a community requires exclusion — and the experience of being excluded from a space one is actively harming is also one that must be designed with care.


## We reject the platform as dictator, to understand the users as distributed citizens with agency

‘Dark patterns’ are where the design of the interface tricks users into a target behavior. They have become the center of a call to action to legislate against the practices of tech companies, as a quintessential example of prioritizing company greed over user needs.

While important and necessary, this discourse still places platforms as the dictator. Through this, it removes agency and responsibility from users. We reject this framing in favor of designing for a **citizen user who is willingly responsible for their own experience** and is empowered to take control over their use of the platform.


## We reject platforms as static, to prioritize using, creating, ignoring, misusing, or even breaking technology with intention

The creation of new technology and platforms is not assumed. **The social problems we are trying to solve do not have an answer in the form of ever-new, ever-created technology.**

While we may decide to craft a new technology, we can also meet people where they already are: on the technology and platforms that they are already using. We can do this because the platforms are not entities beyond our control. We have agency over the ways in which the platform can be utilized. We can build on top of, into, or under the platform. The platform is ever-shifting, and **we must dance with it**.


Technology has never been neutral, and neither has our role as people who choose to create it.
